--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 9 — TRAINING PROCESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

With our 159,000 labelled 512-dimensional embeddings ready, training is
straightforward. We train three independent binary Random Forest classifiers —
one each for Hardhat, Safety Vest, and Gloves — rather than a single
multi-class model. This keeps each classifier focused on a single yes-or-no
decision and prevents imbalance in one category from affecting the others.

The dataset is split 80/20 using stratified sampling, which preserves class
proportions in both subsets — critical for small classes like NO-Safety Vest.
Each Random Forest uses 100 decision trees with unlimited depth and all CPU
cores in parallel. The trees operate on the 512-dimensional embeddings, never
on raw pixels. Because the ResNet encoder already captured the visual semantics,
all three classifiers train in under a minute. The resulting models are saved
to disk as .pkl files.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 10 — INFERENCE PROCESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

At inference, each image passes through a six-step pipeline. First, MediaPipe
attempts to detect 33 body landmarks. If none are found, the image is flagged
as NO POSE DETECTED and skipped.

If a pose is found, four regions are cropped dynamically — head, torso, left
hand, and right hand — with bounding boxes scaled proportionally to the
measured shoulder width, ensuring consistent crops regardless of camera distance.

Each crop is encoded by the frozen ResNet18 into a 512-d vector, then
classified by the corresponding Random Forest. For gloves, a strict AND rule
applies: every visible hand must be classified as gloved for the check to pass.
Finally, the pose skeleton and a colour-coded status panel (green / red / grey)
are drawn onto a saved copy of the image — the original is never modified.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 11 — EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

All three classifiers were evaluated on the 20% held-out validation split.
We report per-class precision, recall, and F1 alongside accuracy, given the
class imbalance.

  Gloves vs NO-Gloves          —  97.3%  (highest)
  Hardhat vs NO-Hardhat        —  94.5%
  Safety Vest vs NO-Safety Vest  —  93.0%  (lowest)

The Gloves classifier leads because gloves produce a highly distinctive
textural signature in the embedding space. The Safety Vest classifier lags,
consistent with its smaller training set (~22,000 samples) and 4:1 class ratio.
Across all three tasks, per-class F1 scores confirm the models are not simply
exploiting majority-class dominance.

On the 87-image real-world test set, pose detection succeeded in roughly 60%
of images. For images where a pose was detected, classification outputs were
visually coherent across varied lighting, angles, and subjects.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 12 — DISCUSSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The results validate the central design premise: a frozen ResNet encoder plus
a trained Random Forest delivers mid-to-high nineties accuracy with no GPU
fine-tuning and sub-minute retraining. This makes the system practical for
organisations that need to update compliance rules without deep learning
infrastructure.

The performance hierarchy — Gloves > Hardhat > Safety Vest — reflects the
discriminability of each item in ResNet's feature space. Gloves have strong
local texture signals; hardhats have consistent shape signals with more colour
variation; safety vests overlap more with ordinary clothing, especially when
crops include background clutter.

The decision to use class_weight=None for the Safety Vest task is a deliberate
simplicity tradeoff. Bagging mitigates but does not fully neutralise the 4:1
imbalance, so the model may be marginally less sensitive to the NO-Safety Vest
class than an explicitly reweighted classifier. This is worth revisiting in
future iterations.

A key advantage of Random Forest over neural alternatives is the native feature
importance ranking across the 512 embedding dimensions — this provides an
auditable explanation of each classification decision, which is valuable in
safety-critical contexts.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 13 — LIMITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Four core limitations bound this system's practical applicability.

First, the entire pipeline depends on successful pose detection. Roughly 40%
of our test images produced no pose — due to missing persons, occlusion, or
encoding errors — yielding no output at all. In live deployment, this becomes
a direct coverage gap.

Second, crop quality is bounded by landmark accuracy. Misdetected landmarks
produce poorly framed crops that may include background clutter or exclude the
PPE item, degrading classifier input quality in ways that are hard to detect
automatically.

Third, a domain shift exists between training and inference. Training crops
come from YOLO bounding box annotations; inference crops come from MediaPipe
landmark positions. These are not identical, and the mismatch is a source of
systematic error that cannot be fully quantified without a purpose-built
benchmark.

Finally, the system has no temporal awareness. Processing isolated frames means
transient occlusions or brief PPE removal may produce unreliable outputs in
video environments, where temporal integration would be essential for
production-grade reliability.
